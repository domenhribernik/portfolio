Same DNA, different renderer

This page imports its flower math straight from the paper one: rosePetals(), bouquetSeats() and the stem Bezier are shared, so a rose opens to the same angles and a bouquet packs into the same dome in both. Everything after the math is a different world, and that difference is the whole experiment.

Paper Flowers (CSS) Wild Flowers (WebGL)
a petal is a flat div wearing a gradient, hinged on its bottom edge; curvature is faked with chained segments a genuinely cupped and curled surface of ~160 triangles, gradient baked into vertex colors
placed by the custom property transform pipeline (--ry --rx --oz --s), one element per plane one instance matrix per petal, computed from the same spec numbers, thousands per draw call
bloom a --bloom variable cascades into every petal's rotateX; each change invalidates the whole scene a uBloom uniform; the vertex shader rotates every petal at its hinge, one float per frame
wind CSS keyframes gently roll whole flowers; turned off on phones because it repaints the scene sin() in the vertex shader: stalks shear by height, heads ride their stalk tips, petals flutter, near zero cost
light painted by hand into every gradient; the shadow under the bouquet is a radial gradient div a warm moon with a real shadow map, a sky hemisphere, and a cornflower fill; petals shade as they turn
the ceiling 12 stems and roughly 520 planes: past that, the compositor's plane splitting turns quadratic and the frame dies 4,000 stems and a couple million triangles on an ordinary laptop; the cost curve is one draw call per part kind
inspect a petal right-click it, it is a div; the whole flower reads in the Elements panel it is three floats in a Float32Array; bring a debugger and a torch

Field notes

An honest scoreboard, because the point of growing the same garden twice was never to declare a winner.

What the paper wins

  • Zero dependencies. This page ships a whole rendering engine from a CDN; that one ships two small files of its own CSS.
  • Every petal is inspectable, stylable, themeable DOM. A colorway there is one line of CSS.
  • Vector crispness at any zoom, no GPU context to lose, nothing to fall back from.
  • The carnation and the dandelion stayed behind on purpose: a fringe mask and a fog of tufts are cheaper to fake with CSS than to mesh honestly.

What the mesh wins

  • Scale. The wrap holds 12 stems; the meadow holds 4,000 and keeps breathing.
  • Real curvature: a rose petal is cupped and curled, not a hinged rectangle squinted at kindly.
  • Light that behaves: shadows fall where the moon says, and a turning petal actually shades.
  • Animation for free: bloom and wind are two floats per frame, whatever the head count. The CSS version pays per plane, every frame.

How to read the meters

  • Watch draw calls stay flat as you raise the stem slider: instancing means more flowers, same number of GPU conversations.
  • Toggle shadows in a 4,000-stem field to feel the depth pass double the vertex work.
  • Flip x-ray to see the triangles the lighting hides, the way the paper page strips its gradients.
  • The 1% low is the honest number: it is what a stutter feels like.
